Capabilities as a
 Cryptographic
Uber-Protocol

Emergent Simplicity

In many domains of engineering, great leverage comes from channeling the power of a set of building block technologies through an intermediate simplifying abstraction before proceeding to build from there. The most compelling example is the digital logic gate. Below this abstraction is the messy and difficult world of analog circuitry. Above this abstraction is the world of digital devices. We know it would be expensive were we to design each digital device -- shift registers, ALUs, etc -- as a separate ad-hoc exercise in analog design. But why do we benefit by imposing this design constraint on ourselves? I propose the answers lie in constrained causality and signal interoperability.

In an analog circuit, influences flow in all sorts of directions. A wire is a bidirectional conductor of causality, but it is also an antenna, transmitting and receiving to other wires. A digital circuit is an analog circuit, so this is still true for a digital circuit. However, in a synchronous digital circuit, in-model causality only flows along authorized pathways. The authorized pathways are the uni-directional view of wires, and in-model causality is a 0 or 1 flowing during a clock cycle, ie, an above-threshold voltage arriving on time. Our design rules are supposed to guarantee that all other analog causality can be safely ignored.

Because our design rules, within a given subsystem, turn voltages into in-model causality in only one way, we know that a 1 emitted from a shift register is also a 1 that can be fed into an ALU. This is signal interoperability. Were each of these components designed separately from analog material, we would always be wrestling with compatibility at the interfaces.

Remote procedure calls, and then distributed object systems, are likewise economizing abstractions for replacing much of the expensive ad-hoc world of cooperative communications protocols. Rather than expensively hand-design a separate cooperative communications protocol for each reason two processes may have to talk to each other, better to expensively design one cooperative communications protocol for transporting inter-object messages. Then, what would have been separate cooperative communications protocols simply become object interface definitions. Given mutual cooperation, causality flows along messages, and object references received via one "protocol" are immediately reusable within another. We call a generic protocol that subsumes the needs for many particular protocols an uber-protocol.

The expense of designing communications protocols is nothing compared to the expense of designing cryptographic protocols (***ref DEC SRC, Schneier), so the economy gained from an uber-protocol should be that much greater. Whereas distributed object protocols constrain causality and provide signal interoperability under a mutual cooperation assumption, a cryptographic uber-protocol must provide its guarantees even when under attack. Pluribus provides such a distributed cryptographic capability uber-protocol. Inter-Vat causality flows only via messages, and only along capabilities, and therefore only along authorized pathways. A capability received via a message of one object interface is immediately usable in any other message.

Why is signal interoperability important? Consider the likely world of cryptographic commerce in the absence of a cryptographic uber-protocol. X works on a cryptographic protocol for running an auction. Y and Z work on two different systems from cryptographic money. W works out a cryptographic escrow exchange agent. Let's say they all do a locally perfect job. Should we expect that the money obtained via Y's protocol will be accepted by X's auction? Or that electronic rights purchased through X's auction will be the kind of rights W's escrow exchange protocol knows how to escrow? An uber-protocol doesn't make this problem trivial, any more than object libraries are trivially reusable! However, in the absence of an uber-protocol, we would have the equivalent of the normal reuse problem on top of the equivalent of the inter-language interoperability problem.

But is this a Cryptographic Uber-Protocol?

The design of cryptographic protocols are generally much harder problems than the design of cooperative protocols, as they must work under all sorts of mutual-suspicion constraints. Also, they are a larger design space, because many are built on strange inventions for mapping obscure mathematical properties into very specific mistrust arrangements. Therefore, we are unlikely to find cryptographic uber protocol as comprehensive as the cooperative uber-protocols so far discovered.

Indeed, although this paper claims a cryptographic implementation of the Granovetter Operator (such as Pluribus) is a cryptographic uber-protocol, there are a number of useful cryptographic protocol design ideas that it does not subsume. Nevertheless, this one protocol subsumes such a large range of stock cryptographic protocols that we should treat it as the 80%-solution-framework onto which we should layer other protocols as needed.
